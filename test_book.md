# The Art of Clear Thinking

## Chapter 1: The First Principle

The beginning of all skill is understanding the terrain. Before we can build anything of value, we must first see what is actually there — not what we wish were there, not what we have been told is there, but the raw, unvarnished facts of the situation.

This is harder than it sounds. The human mind is a pattern-matching engine, and it will find patterns even where none exist. We see faces in clouds, meaning in random noise, and causality in mere correlation. The first discipline of clear thinking is the willingness to say: I do not know yet.

## Chapter 2: Slow Thinking

There are two systems at work in every decision. System One is fast, instinctive, and emotional. System Two is slow, deliberate, and logical. Most of us spend most of our time in System One, even when System Two would serve us better.

The trick is not to eliminate System One — it is fast for a reason, and many of its judgements are sound. The trick is to know when to call upon System Two, and to build the habit of doing so before the consequences of a decision become irreversible.

## Chapter 3: Reversibility

Jeff Bezos popularised the idea of one-way and two-way doors. A two-way door is a decision you can walk back from. A one-way door is harder to undo. The discipline is simple: most decisions should be treated as two-way doors, and you should move fast. Only the rare one-way door deserves the full weight of deliberation.

Most of the anxiety we feel about decisions comes from treating two-way doors as though they were one-way. The cure is a clear-eyed assessment of what reversibility actually looks like in practice.
